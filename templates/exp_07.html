Displacement Reaction — Zinc and Copper Sulphate

Learn about the displacement reaction between zinc and copper sulphate and what it reveals about the reactivity of metals.

📋

Introduction

A displacement reaction is a type of chemical reaction where a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound in solution. In this experiment, we will explore the displacement reaction between zinc metal and copper sulphate solution. Zinc is higher than copper in the reactivity series, so it is able to displace copper ions from the solution. The reaction produces zinc sulphate and deposits solid copper metal, and is represented by the equation:

Zn (s) + CuSO₄ (aq) → ZnSO₄ (aq) + Cu (s)

🔬

Experiment Overview

In this experiment, zinc metal will be added to a blue copper sulphate solution. As the reaction proceeds, the blue colour of the copper sulphate solution will fade as copper ions are displaced and zinc sulphate (which is colourless in solution) forms in their place. A reddish-brown deposit of solid copper metal will appear on the surface of the zinc. This experiment directly demonstrates the reactivity series of metals and how a more reactive metal can displace a less reactive one from its salt solution.

⚗️

Procedure

  • Put on your safety goggles and gloves before handling any chemicals.
  • Pour approximately 50 ml of copper sulphate solution into a clean beaker or test tube.
  • Observe and record the initial blue colour of the copper sulphate solution.
  • Using tongs or forceps, add a small piece or a few granules of zinc metal into the solution.
  • Observe the reaction — note any colour changes in the solution and on the surface of the zinc metal.
  • Allow the reaction to proceed for 5–10 minutes, stirring gently with a stirring rod occasionally.
  • Record all observations, including the colour change in the solution and the appearance of any solid deposit on the zinc.
🔍

Observations

As zinc metal is added to the copper sulphate solution, the characteristic blue colour of the solution gradually fades. This happens because copper(II) ions are being removed from the solution as they are displaced by zinc. A reddish-brown solid — metallic copper — begins to deposit on the surface of the zinc. By the end of the reaction, the solution will have turned colourless (zinc sulphate) and a visible layer of copper metal will coat the zinc surface, confirming that the displacement reaction has taken place.

💡

Real-World Applications

  • Galvanisation: Iron and steel are coated with zinc to prevent rusting. Since zinc is more reactive, it corrodes preferentially, protecting the iron beneath — a direct application of the reactivity series.
  • Metal extraction: In metallurgy, more reactive metals are used to displace less reactive metals from their ores during extraction processes.
  • Electroplating: Displacement reactions are used to deposit thin layers of metal onto surfaces for decorative or protective purposes.
  • Metal recycling: Displacement is used industrially to recover valuable metals like copper or silver from waste solutions.
⚠️

Safety Precautions

  • Wear safety goggles and gloves throughout the experiment — copper sulphate is toxic and should not contact skin or eyes.
  • Handle zinc metal with tongs or forceps rather than bare hands.
  • Conduct the experiment in a well-ventilated area and avoid inhaling any dust from the chemicals.
  • Do not pour copper sulphate solution down the drain — dispose of it according to your school or lab's chemical waste guidelines.
  • Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water after completing the experiment.

Summary

    Notes

In this experiment, we confirmed that zinc is more reactive than copper by observing a classic displacement reaction. Zinc displaced copper ions from the copper sulphate solution, causing the blue colour to fade as colourless zinc sulphate formed and reddish-brown copper metal was deposited on the zinc surface.

    Common Misconceptions

A common misconception is that any metal can displace any other metal from its solution. In reality, displacement only occurs when the reacting metal is higher in the reactivity series than the metal in the compound. Copper cannot displace zinc from zinc sulphate because copper is less reactive than zinc.

    Key Takeaways
  • Displacement reactions occur when a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.
  • The reactivity series is the key reference for predicting whether a displacement reaction will occur.
  • Observable evidence of a displacement reaction includes a colour change in the solution and a solid metal deposit forming on the reacting metal.
  • This principle has practical applications in galvanisation, metal extraction, and electroplating.

The Reactivity Series in Action

Displacement reactions like the one between zinc and copper sulphate are a direct demonstration of the reactivity series of metals. A more reactive metal will always displace a less reactive one from its solution. Understanding this principle is key to topics like metal extraction, corrosion prevention, and electrochemistry.
