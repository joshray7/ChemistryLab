States of Matter

Matter exists in different physical forms. Explore the three fundamental states — solid, liquid, and gas — and how they transform into one another.

🧊
Solid
State I

Definite Shape & Volume

Solids have a definite shape and volume. The particles are closely packed in a regular arrangement and vibrate in fixed positions — they cannot move freely.

Fixed shape Fixed volume High density Low compressibility
💧
Liquid
State II

Definite Volume, No Fixed Shape

Liquids have a definite volume but no fixed shape. They take the shape of whatever container holds them. Particles are close but can slide past each other.

No fixed shape Fixed volume Flows freely Slight compressibility
💨
Gas
State III

No Fixed Shape or Volume

Gases have neither a fixed shape nor a fixed volume. Particles move freely and rapidly, spreading out to fill any container they occupy completely.

No fixed shape No fixed volume Fills container Highly compressible
🔄

Changes of State

→

Melting

Solid absorbs heat and particles gain enough energy to break free of fixed positions.

Solid ▶ Liquid
→

Freezing

Liquid loses heat and particles slow down until they lock into fixed positions.

Liquid ▶ Solid
→

Evaporation

Liquid gains heat and surface particles escape into the air as gas.

Liquid ▶ Gas
→

Condensation

Gas loses heat and particles slow down, coming together to form a liquid.

Gas ▶ Liquid
Note: Sublimation (solid to gas) and deposition (gas to solid) are additional changes of state that occur under specific conditions.

Understanding states of matter helps explain everyday physical and chemical changes.
